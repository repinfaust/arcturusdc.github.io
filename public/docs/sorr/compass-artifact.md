# SoRR Control fills a real gap in a fast-moving market

**A governed orchestration layer that classifies AI requests, resolves policies, and routes to tiered paths is not something you can buy off the shelf today — and the evidence strongly supports building one.** No existing tool combines request-level classification, organizational policy resolution, multi-path routing (immediate/approval/governed workspace), and cross-tool governance for environments spanning Claude, Cowork, and MCP-connected agents. The market has pieces — content guardrails, model-level governance platforms, LLM gateways, approval workflow engines — but none stitch these into the unified control plane SoRR describes. Meanwhile, analyst data from Gartner, Forrester, McKinsey, and Deloitte overwhelmingly shows that governance done right **accelerates** AI deployment rather than constraining it: organizations with mature governance deploy **40% faster** and are **3.4x more likely** to scale AI successfully. The business case is not governance versus speed — it is governance as the prerequisite for sustainable speed.

## The market has four quadrants, and SoRR sits at their intersection

The off-the-shelf landscape divides into four categories, each covering part of what SoRR Control proposes but none covering the whole.

**AI governance and policy platforms** (Credo AI, Holistic AI, Arthur AI) operate at the model and system lifecycle level. They excel at regulatory compliance documentation, AI asset inventory, risk assessment, and audit-ready artifacts. Credo AI — named a Leader in the Forrester Wave for AI Governance Solutions Q3 2025 — accelerated one customer's EU AI Act compliance by **10x** over manual processes. Holistic AI discovers shadow AI in codebases and classifies systems against the EU AI Act's risk tiers. Arthur AI provides real-time model monitoring with drift detection and fairness checks. But these platforms answer "are our AI *systems* governed?" not "is this specific *employee request* being handled appropriately?" None classifies individual requests by business sensitivity, routes to approval workflows, or enforces per-request policy bundles combining user identity, data classification, and action type.

**LLMOps and agent orchestration tools** (LangSmith, Langfuse, Portkey, Braintrust) focus on observability, evaluation, and operational routing. **Portkey is the closest existing tool** to SoRR's concept — it's a Gartner Cool Vendor handling **10B+ requests/month** with input/output guardrails, multi-model routing across 1,600+ LLMs, PII detection, RBAC, and a new MCP Gateway feature providing centralized control for MCP servers with auth and access controls. However, Portkey routes based on *technical* criteria (model selection, cost, latency) not *business/policy* criteria (data sensitivity, organizational rules, compliance requirements). It cannot hold a request pending human approval or route to a governed workspace based on data classification. LangSmith and Langfuse are purely observability tools with no policy enforcement. Braintrust focuses on evaluation and quality, not governance.

**Cloud provider guardrails** (AWS Bedrock Guardrails, Azure AI Content Safety, Google Vertex AI) provide strong content safety filtering within their respective ecosystems. AWS Bedrock now enables **IAM policy-based enforcement** of mandatory guardrails across AWS Organizations — security teams can enforce content filters, PII detection, and grounding checks on all model inference calls. Azure's Content Safety service offers severity scoring across harm categories with custom category support. Google's Cloud API Registry in Vertex AI Agent Builder lets administrators curate approved tools for developers. The critical limitation is that **all three classify by harm category, not business sensitivity**. They cannot determine "this question about revenue forecasts should route through CFO approval" or "this involves client data requiring the restricted workspace." They also lock governance to a single cloud ecosystem, which fails in multi-model, multi-tool environments.

**Approval workflow tools** (ServiceNow AI Control Tower, Workato) have the most mature governance *engines* but lack AI request intelligence. ServiceNow's AI Control Tower — launched May 2025 — is a centralized hub for governing AI agents with threshold-based escalation, risk categorization, and human-in-the-loop decisioning. Deal volume **quadrupled quarter-over-quarter** and AI products approached **$500M in ACV**. Workato's Enterprise MCP platform provides authentication, granular permissions, and traceability for AI agent access. But both tools govern AI *within their own ecosystems* — ServiceNow manages its agents and workflows, Workato manages its integration automations. Neither intercepts and classifies arbitrary employee requests to external AI tools like Claude or MCP-connected agents.

**The specific gap SoRR fills** is the absence of a unified request-level orchestration layer combining AI request intelligence (what does this request contain?), organizational policy resolution (user role × data classification × action type → policy bundle), multi-path routing with human-in-the-loop (immediate/approval/governed workspace), and cross-tool governance across Claude, Cowork, and MCP agents. No existing tool delivers all four. The closest approximation would require stitching Portkey (gateway routing) + ServiceNow (approval workflows) + custom classification logic — substantial custom engineering that essentially *is* building SoRR.

## Shadow AI is near-universal, and governance paradoxically accelerates adoption

The adoption evidence challenges every intuition about governance as friction. Understanding the current state of shadow AI explains why.

**Shadow AI is not an edge case — it is the default.** Research from multiple independent sources converges on a striking picture: **98%** of organizations have employees using unsanctioned AI apps, **80%+** of workers use unapproved AI tools, **78%** bring their own AI tools to work (Microsoft WorkLab 2024), and **68%** use personal free-tier accounts (Menlo Security 2025). Even **90% of security professionals** use unauthorized AI tools (UpGuard 2025). Perhaps most telling: only **18.5%** of employees are even aware of their company's AI policy (2025 survey of 12,000+ workers). This is not a behavioral problem — it is a structural visibility problem, amplified in SaaS environments where AI features get embedded inside tools employees already use without explicit rollout decisions.

The financial consequences are severe. Shadow AI breaches add **$670,000** to average breach costs — a **16% increase** — according to IBM's Cost of a Data Breach report. **46%** of organizations reported internal data leaks through generative AI (Cisco 2025). **97%** of AI-related breaches lacked proper access controls. Stanford's HAI AI Index documented **233 AI-related incidents** involving governance failures in 2024 alone. The average enterprise hosts approximately **1,200 unofficial AI applications**, and AI-native app spending surged **108% in 2025** to an average of **$1.2M per organization**, much of it uncontrolled.

**The governance paradox is real and well-documented.** A Gartner Q2 2025 survey of 360 organizations found those deploying AI governance platforms are **3.4x more likely to achieve high effectiveness** in AI governance. The 2025 AI Governance Benchmark Report found organizations with mature governance frameworks deploy AI **40% faster** and achieve **30% better ROI**. Gartner predicts that by 2026, organizations operationalizing AI transparency and security will see **50% improvement** in AI adoption, business goals, and user acceptance. McKinsey's State of AI 2025 found that the top 6% of AI high-performers — where AI contributes more than 5% of EBIT — have *more* governance, not less, and are **3.6x more likely** to pursue transformational change.

**Time-to-value data reveals the "enterprise paradox."** Foundational AI governance programs take **4-6 months** to implement (Liminal 2025), with assessment and planning consuming 4-6 weeks, policy development 8-10 weeks, and technical controls 6-8 weeks. Large enterprises take up to **9 months** to move from pilot to production, while mid-market firms accomplish this in approximately **90 days**. The bottleneck is not technology — it is organizational friction. Governance-as-code and risk-tiered approaches collapse these timelines dramatically, with purpose-built platforms reducing implementation time by **30-50%** versus custom solutions. McKinsey's 2025 data explicitly recommends establishing an approvals matrix by risk tier, pre-approving tools and datasets, and logging prompts and outputs — precisely what SoRR Control automates.

The most critical adoption success patterns for SaaS companies include treating governance as an enabler rather than a gatekeeper, implementing risk-tiered controls where low-risk requests flow freely, embedding governance into developer workflows rather than layering it on top, and starting with co-designed progressive rollouts that demonstrate quick wins. Organizations that **provide approved alternatives** to shadow AI see reduced unauthorized usage — banning tools only drives usage underground. As one JumpCloud analysis noted, "shadow AI is the #1 indicator of unmet business needs."

## How to pitch it: security team first, then reframe for the CPO

The pitch sequence matters because security teams are natural first champions for AI governance, and their endorsement transforms the CPO conversation from "should we govern?" to "how fast can we deploy this?"

**For the CISO and security team, four risk frames resonate most powerfully.** First, *the visibility gap*: 86% of organizations have no visibility into AI data flows, and only 21% of executives report complete visibility into agent permissions, tool usage, or data access patterns. "You can't secure what you can't see" is the opening line. Second, *agent autonomy as expanded blast radius*: MCP-connected agents can browse the web, execute code, access files, and interact with multiple enterprise systems — a single prompt injection becomes a multi-system incident. Security researchers have documented confused deputy attacks, tool poisoning (where malicious tool descriptions serve as hidden agent instructions), supply chain attacks through fake MCP server packages, and scope inflation where broad permissions granted during pilots never get tightened. Two-thirds of open-source MCP servers show critical vulnerabilities per Docker's analysis. Third, *shadow AI as shadow IT on steroids*: after strict bans, hidden AI use rises by up to 40%, with tools holding OAuth access to email, CRM, and code repositories. Fourth, *compliance as a forcing function*: NIST AI RMF, ISO 42001, EU AI Act, OWASP Top 10 for Agentic AI (published December 2025), and evolving SOC 2 requirements all demand demonstrable AI controls. The CISO pitch should frame SoRR as extending existing security controls — IAM, DLP, audit logging, SIEM — into the AI layer, not adding a new process.

**For the CPO, the reframe hinges on a single evidence-backed argument: a governed fast lane is always faster than ungoverned chaos followed by incident-driven lockdown.** The Samsung trajectory — uncontrolled adoption → data leak → blanket ban → shadow AI proliferation → eventually build governed platform anyway — costs 3-6 months of lost productivity and competitive positioning. IBM's governed environment went from a two-week business case process to **five or six minutes** for environment provisioning. A healthcare system that provided approved AI tools saw **89% reduction** in unauthorized use *and* **32 minutes** daily time savings per clinician. The language shift matters: "compliance requirement" becomes **"deployment accelerator,"** "security review" becomes **"automated quality gate,"** "policy enforcement" becomes **"golden path to production."** The core message: "The bottleneck isn't governance — it's the *absence* of governance. When rules aren't clear, every team reinvents, every deployment requires a bespoke security review, and the first incident triggers a lockdown that kills productivity for months."

**Navigating the security-product dynamic requires a centralized-federated model** — the consensus best practice from Databricks, Google Cloud's CISO office, and IBM. A central team defines standards, risk frameworks, and policies; domain and product teams apply them locally and remain accountable for outcomes. Risk tiering is the critical mechanism: low-risk AI requests get auto-approved through the fast lane, medium-risk requests go through streamlined review, and only high-risk requests require full committee review. This directly maps to SoRR's three-path routing (immediate/approval-required/governed workspace) and is how organizations like OneAdvanced — which repositioned security from gatekeeper to enabler after a 2022 cyberattack — embedded governance across product teams through Security Champions who translate policy into practical decision-making.

The internal champion path follows a clear escalation: security team buy-in through a pilot on 3-5 highest-risk AI use cases → CISO sponsorship with documented risk reduction → cross-functional AI governance committee formation (security, privacy, legal, product, engineering) → executive presentation framing governance as competitive advantage. For the CFO: governance costs 0.5-1% of AI spend; a single incident costs 10-100x that annually. For the board: organizations with governance deploy AI 40% faster and achieve 30% better ROI.

## Build versus buy, and where to start

The build-versus-buy calculus for SoRR Control tilts toward building a thin custom orchestration layer that integrates existing commercial components rather than building everything from scratch or buying a single platform.

**What to buy:** Use Portkey or a similar AI gateway for multi-model routing, content guardrails, cost tracking, and MCP server management — it handles the technical plumbing well. Leverage your existing approval workflow engine (ServiceNow, Jira, or equivalent) for human-in-the-loop flows. Adopt a governance platform like Credo AI or Holistic AI if regulatory compliance documentation is a near-term requirement.

**What to build:** The custom layer is the *policy resolution engine* — the component that takes a request, classifies it by business sensitivity using your organization's data classification scheme, resolves the applicable policy bundle (user role × data classification × tool capability × action type), and routes to the appropriate path. This is inherently organization-specific because it encodes your particular data sensitivity rules, approval hierarchies, and risk tolerances. Build the request classifier, the policy resolver, and the routing logic. Wire it to Portkey for gateway execution, your workflow tool for approvals, and your logging infrastructure for audit trails.

**Where to start:** Pilot with the highest-risk, most visible use case — likely AI agents accessing live ServiceNow or billing systems via MCP. Demonstrate that the governance layer catches requests that should require approval, routes routine queries instantly, and produces audit logs the security team can review. This simultaneously proves value to the security team (visibility and control), the product team (fast lane for safe requests), and leadership (measurable reduction in ungoverned AI risk). Then expand to additional tools and teams, using the pilot data to build the business case for broader rollout.

The market timing favors action. The AI governance market is growing at **35-51% CAGR**, the demand for request-level orchestration is emerging but no dominant solution exists, and every month of ungoverned AI usage accumulates risk and technical debt. The organizations that build governance infrastructure now will compound their advantage as AI agents become more capable and more deeply integrated with live business systems throughout 2026 and beyond.
